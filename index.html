Global Alcohol Consumption and Interesting Point



Alcohol, a substance with a rich history, has deeply rooted itself in various facets of human civilization. Since ancient times, alcohol has played a pivotal role in numerous social, religious, and cultural activities. Whether it's celebrated as a mark of triumph, incorporated into religious ceremonies, or merely consumed as part of daily life for relaxation, alcohol has held significant stature throughout human history.

This map showcases the annual alcohol consumption rates across different countries worldwide. Not only does it offer insights into the drinking cultures of various nations, but it also reveals the interplay between consumption patterns and economic, health, and social factors. Users can utilize the filter on the left to select and view the alcohol consumption rates for countries in different regions.



Now that we have observed the annual alcohol consumption rates globally, let's delve into whether there's a direct correlation between alcohol consumption and the economy. The answer is, Yes. Users can discern from the scatter plot on the left that there's generally a positive correlationbetween GDP and alcohol consumption. In more economically developed regions, people tend to consume more alcohol.

(This graph's X-axis represents the annual alcohol consumption, and the Y-axis represents the GDP.)






As we all know, the economies of the United States and the United Kingdom are very developed, but what about their alcohol consumption?







The chart on the left is an area river graph, which displays the volume of alcohol (in liters) consumed by Americans in different venues from 1990 to 2014. Different colors represent different venues. Users can drag the slider to see more details.

The chart on the right is a rose chart, showing the alcohol consumption in the UK across different income groups over the years. Interestingly, we can see that the higher the income, the more alcohol is consumed.

Overall, we can observe that both Americans and Brits really enjoy drinking.





Dangers of Alcohol



After discussing the relaxation that alcohol can bring, it's essential to mention that excessive drinking can also cause severe harm to the body. The density chart on the left displays the number of sudden deaths per million people resulting from a specific volume of alcohol consumed. The X-axis represents the number of deaths, while the Y-axis indicates the amount of alcohol consumed. We can observe that the more alcohol consumed, the higher the probability of sudden death.

However, not all drinking is for relaxation. The radar chart on the right presents the most common psychological reasons for drinking. The numbers represent the multiplier for alcohol addiction. If you identify with one of these reasons, your likelihood of alcohol dependency is multiplied by that number.

In summary, while drinking can be relaxing, it's crucial not to get addicted!
